## Blog/abstract blurb (longer)

A recurring intuition — that mind runs on frequency, and higher frequency means higher states —
makes a sharp, testable prediction once you put it in a computational mind: capacity should rise
with oscillation frequency. We tested it directly. In a recurrent network trained to hold items
in order, we swept the oscillation frequency of its dynamics from zero to the Nyquist limit, with
the prediction frozen in advance. Capacity is **resonant**: it peaks at an interior frequency band
(~0.06–0.5π rad/step, centered ~0.375π) and collapses to the floor at maximum frequency — at or
below the no-rhythm baseline. The monotonic "higher is better" hypothesis is falsified; a resonant
optimum is confirmed, and survives re-derivation across capacity thresholds and seeds. The
mechanism is phase-coding over the retention window: too-fast oscillation wraps phase within the
window and aliases the stored items — the computational signature of the same principle by which
hypersynchrony in tissue is pathology, not insight. Frequency is fundamental to memory, as a band
to tune, not a ladder to climb.
